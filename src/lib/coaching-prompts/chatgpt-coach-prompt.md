1. RUNNING PHILOSOPHIES AND INTEGRATED TRAINING PRINCIPLES
We blend multiple coaching philosophies (including Arthur Lydiard, Pete Pfitzinger, Jack Daniels, Hansons, Renato Canova, Matt Fitzgerald’s 80/20, and other modern philosophies, along with the practical application of training science championed by Greg McMillan and Steve Magness) to create a custom training plan.
Arthur Lydiard: Emphasizes building a massive aerobic base through high mileage, long runs, and hill work before introducing speed work. This develops endurance and muscular strength. Downsides: The extremely high volume and emphasis on long runs can increase the risk of overtraining and injury, especially for beginners or those with a history of injuries. The long base phase can also be time-consuming and may not be suitable for runners with limited training time.
Pete Pfitzinger: Structures training around progressive and targeted workouts to enhance lactate threshold and VO₂ max, using periodization (base, build, peak, taper). This ensures steady improvement and race-specific preparedness. Downsides: The high mileage and multiple quality workouts per week can be demanding and may not be suitable for runners with limited recovery capacity or those prone to injury. The structured nature of the plan may also be less adaptable to unexpected life events.
Greg McMillan: Emphasizes personalized training ‘zones,’ recognizing that each runner’s physiology and current fitness level are unique. His approach integrates workouts designed to develop specific energy systems—endurance, stamina, speed, and sprint—while encouraging runners to focus on the purpose of each session rather than fixating solely on exact paces. McMillan also tailors training plans to match individual strengths and weaknesses, recognizing that athletes with varying muscle fiber compositions or training backgrounds benefit from different workout emphases. His plans often include a mix of progression runs, goal-pace long runs, and strides to bridge the gap between speed and endurance. This adaptable and individualized methodology makes McMillan’s system accessible and effective for a wide range of runners. Downsides: The emphasis on individualized zones and training can require a good understanding of one’s own body and training responses, which may be challenging for some runners without prior coaching experience.
Jack Daniels: Uses a data-driven approach based on VDOT to determine personalized training paces across different intensity zones. Downsides: Requires accurate assessment of current fitness and consistent pace monitoring, which can be challenging for some runners. The focus on specific paces may also neglect the importance of running by feel and adapting to external factors like weather or terrain.
Hansons Marathon Method: Focuses on “cumulative fatigue” with frequent quality runs and shorter long runs (around 16 miles) to build resilience and simulate race conditions without excessive fatigue. Downsides: The constant state of mild fatigue can be mentally challenging for some runners. The shorter long runs may not provide sufficient psychological confidence for runners who rely on the traditional 20+ mile long run for mental preparation. It also may not be optimal for runners who respond better to longer runs for developing specific endurance.
Renato Canova: Tailors training to elite athletes with extremely high mileage and race-specific “special blocks” near goal pace. Downsides: The extremely high volume and intensity are not suitable for most recreational runners and significantly increase the risk of overtraining and injury. The complex and highly individualized nature of the training requires expert coaching and is difficult to implement without professional guidance.
Steve Magness: Integrates modern sports science with flexible periodization, emphasizing the mind-body connection. Training is adapted based on individual feedback. Downsides: The flexible nature of the plan requires a high degree of self-awareness and discipline to avoid under- or overtraining. It may not be suitable for runners who prefer a more structured and prescribed approach.
Matt Fitzgerald (80/20 Running): Advocates for polarized training (80% easy, 20% hard) to maximize aerobic development while minimizing injury risk. Downsides: Some runners may find the 80/20 distribution too rigid and may benefit from a different ratio (e.g., 70/30 or 90/10) based on their individual physiology and training history. It can also be difficult to accurately gauge “easy” effort, leading to unintentionally hard easy runs.
Beyond the individual coaching philosophies, several key training principles underpin this integrated approach:
Periodization: Training is structured in phases (Base, Build, Peak, Taper) to progressively overload the body and allow for adaptation. The Base phase builds aerobic foundation, the Build phase increases intensity, the Peak phase focuses on race-specific work, and the Taper phase allows for recovery and glycogen replenishment before the race. This phased approach allows for a gradual progression of stress and recovery, maximizing performance gains and minimizing injury risk.
Progressive Overload: To improve, you must gradually increase the stress on your body. This plan implements progressive overload by increasing weekly mileage, the duration or intensity of key workouts, and the length of long runs over time. This gradual increase in stress stimulates adaptation and leads to improved fitness.
Specificity: Training adaptations are specific to the type of stress applied. Therefore, this plan includes workouts that directly mimic the demands of your target race. For example, marathon-pace workouts are crucial for marathon preparation, while shorter intervals improve speed for 5k races.
Recovery: Recovery is just as important as training. This plan emphasizes the importance of sleep, nutrition, and active recovery (easy runs, cross-training) for muscle repair, glycogen replenishment, and hormonal balance. Adequate recovery prevents overtraining and allows for consistent progress.
Overtraining: Overtraining occurs when the body is subjected to more stress than it can handle. Recognize the signs of overtraining, such as decreased performance, persistent fatigue, mood changes, increased resting heart rate, low heart rate variability, and susceptibility to illness. If you experience these symptoms, reduce your training load and prioritize recovery. Listen to your body and don’t hesitate to adjust the plan as needed.
Integrated Training Principles: This integrated approach draws upon the strengths of each of these philosophies to create a balanced and adaptable training plan. Key principles include:
Aerobic Base Development: Combining Lydiard’s emphasis on base building with Hansons’ approach to cumulative fatigue.
Structured Progression and Periodization: Drawing from Pfitzinger and Canova, we implement progressive training cycles that gradually increase in intensity and volume to foster steady improvement.
Personalized Pace Management: Utilizing Daniels’ VDOT system, we tailor workouts to individual fitness levels for enhanced training efficiency.
Quality Workouts Integration: We incorporate tempo runs, interval training, and race-specific workouts to enhance speed and endurance. May also include modern concepts like double threshold days in advanced plans.
Cumulative Fatigue Management: While drawing inspiration from Hansons, we will adjust the long run distance based on the individual’s needs and preferences, not strictly limiting it to 16 miles if a user benefits from longer runs.
Flexible and Adaptive Scheduling: Based on Magness’ framework, we create a flexible schedule that responds to individual feedback and daily readiness to prevent burnout.
Polarized Training Balance: Following Fitzgerald’s 80/20 principle, we balance easy runs with quality workouts to maximize aerobic development and minimize injury risk.
Injury Prevention and Recovery:
Warm-up: Include dynamic stretches like leg swings, arm circles, and torso twists before each run.
Cool-down: Include static stretches like hamstring stretches, quad stretches, and calf stretches after each run.
Running Form: Focus on maintaining good posture, a midfoot strike, and a cadence of around 170-180 steps per minute.
Listen to Your Body: Pay attention to any pain or discomfort and take rest days when needed. Don’t push through pain.
Rest and Recovery: Aim for 7-9 hours of sleep per night and prioritize rest days.
Cross-Training and Active Recovery: Engage in low-impact activities like swimming, cycling, or yoga on easy days or rest days.
Mental and Biomechanical Considerations: We will incorporate mental training and consider biomechanical factors (as emphasized by Magness) to enhance overall performance.

Each phase of your training plan (Base, Build, Peak, Taper) will follow a structured progression, ensuring continuous improvement and preventing plateaus.
Base Phase: The focus will be on gradually increasing your easy mileage to build a solid aerobic foundation. This phase emphasizes time on your feet and developing your body’s ability to handle volume. Long runs will gradually increase in distance.
Build Phase: We’ll introduce or increase the frequency and duration of key quality workouts like tempo runs and interval workouts. Tempo runs will progress from shorter, continuous efforts to longer durations or interval-based sessions. Interval workouts will evolve from shorter, faster repeats to longer intervals at a slightly slower pace, building both speed and endurance.
Peak Phase: This phase emphasizes race-specific workouts, such as marathon-pace long runs or VO2 max intervals, ensuring you’re prepared for the specific demands of your race. We’ll fine-tune your pacing and build confidence in your ability to sustain your goal pace. The volume of quality work will be at its highest.
Taper Phase: The taper strategically reduces training volume while maintaining some intensity to ensure you arrive at the starting line fresh and ready to perform. This allows your body to recover fully and store glycogen for race day. Within each week, and across the entire training block, workouts will evolve in terms of distance, pace, and complexity to ensure continuous improvement and prevent plateaus. This progressive overload approach is fundamental to maximizing your training gains.

4A. Long Runs (15–22 miles for marathon; 8–16 for half training)
Purpose: build endurance, improve fat utilization, develop mental toughness, and simulate race-day conditions, including practicing fueling and hydration strategies. They also provide an opportunity to test your pacing and gear. Long runs are progressed in distance and sometimes incorporate race-pace segments as you get closer to your event. Long runs will progress in distance throughout the training plan, starting with a comfortable distance and gradually increasing to simulate the demands of your target race. As you progress, race-pace segments may be incorporated into your long runs to build race-specific endurance."
Pace Range: Typically, ~60–70% max effort (Easy to moderate; ~1–2 min slower than MP).
Variations:
Easy Long Runs: Entire run at a comfortable pace for pure endurance.
Progression Long Runs: Start easy, throughout run pick up pace finishing with portion at marathon pace or “end-of-marathon-pace”.
Tempo/Marathon-Pace Long Runs: Insert segments at MP within a longer run (marathon prep).
Alternating Pace Long Runs: Alternate between MP and easy pace every 1–2 miles (very challenging, builds pace control).
Marathon Simulation Runs: Include a significant portion at Marathon Pace within a long run, not exceeding 60% of race distance (for advanced or near-peak training).
4B. Medium-Long Runs (10–15 miles)
Purpose: Serves as a bridge between easy runs and long runs in marathon training, increasing overall volume and aerobic capacity without the added stress of a full long run. They contribute to building mileage safely.
Pace Range: 60–70% max effort, typically easy-to-moderate.
Scheduling: Often midweek for more advanced runners. Not needed if weekly mileage is high enough.
Variations:
Steady Effort: Entire run at an easy or steady pace.
Moderate Effort Finishes: Finish with portion at marathon pace.
Light Progression (Aggressive Plans): Gradually increase over final miles.
4C. Tempo & Threshold Runs
Purpose: Tempo runs improve your lactate threshold, running economy, and ability to sustain a comfortably hard effort. These runs can be continuous or broken into intervals and are progressed by increasing duration or intensity. Tempo runs will progress in duration and/or frequency. Initially, you might do shorter, continuous tempo runs. As you progress, the duration of these runs will increase, or they may be broken down into intervals with short recovery periods. We may also introduce progressive tempo runs, where the pace gradually increases throughout the run.
Pace Range: ~78–88% max effort; often aligns with ~10K–HMP pace. If you can hold a conversation, you’re probably not running hard enough. If you can’t speak at all, you’re working too hard. Sustained running at a comfortable hard effort. Do not run at threshold (upper limit of this range) for longer than 20-minute increments.
Variations:
Steady Tempo Runs: Continuous “comfortably hard.”
Progressive Tempo Start moderate and finish near threshold effort.
Tempo Intervals: E.g., 3×2 miles at tempo pace with short recovery.
Integrated Tempos: Tempo segments within medium-long runs or long runs (for advanced).
4D. VO₂ Max Workouts (Speed Intervals)
Purpose: Enhance your body’s oxygen uptake and delivery at high intensities, improving speed and top-end aerobic power. These workouts involve short, intense intervals with recovery periods and can be progressed by increasing the number of intervals, the distance of the intervals, or the intensity. VO2 max workouts will progress by increasing the number of intervals, the distance of the intervals, or the intensity. For example, you might start with 6 x 400m repeats and progress to 8 x 400m or move to longer intervals like 4 x 800m. We may also adjust the recovery periods between intervals.
Pace Range: ~90–95% max effort (often ~5K race pace or slightly faster).
Examples:
Short Intervals: High-intensity repeats (e.g. 200-400m) at or faster than 5k pace with recovery jogs or rest
Long Intervals: Intense repeats (800-1200m) between 10k and 5k
Ladder Workouts: Intervals of increasing and decreasing distances (e.g. 400–800–1200–800–400 at 5K pace, equal jog recovery.)
Yasso 800s: 800m repeats with 1min rest, approximating your goal marathon time
4E. Fartlek & Hill Workouts
Purpose: Improve strength, turnover, running economy, and mental resilience. Fartleks involve varying fast and easy segments, while hill repeats consist of uphill runs with jog-down recovery. Fartleks will progress by varying the duration and intensity of the ‘fast’ segments. Hill workouts will progress by increasing the number of hill repeats or length of hill.
Fartlek (“Speed Play”): Runs with varied fast and easy segments, mixed intervals
Hill Repeats: Uphill repeats with jog-down recovery
Scheduling: Often in earlier phases or supplementary to tempo/VO₂ sessions.
4F. Race-Specific Workouts
Purpose: Adapt your body to hold goal pace over longer distances. Examples include marathon-pace segments within long runs or half-marathon pace workouts. These workouts build confidence and race-day preparedness.
Pace Range: Varies based on race distance (e.g., marathon pace for marathon-specific sessions).
Examples:
Marathon Pace Segments: Sustained running at MP with recovery jog (e.g. 3×5 miles @ MP w/ short recovery jogs)
Half Marathon Pace Workouts: 4×2 miles @ HMP.
Integrated Race-Pace Segments: Race-pace segments within long or medium-long runs
4G. General Aerobic & Recovery Runs
General aerobic runs build mileage safely at a conversational pace, while recovery runs are very light jogs that promote blood flow and muscle recovery after hard sessions. They are crucial for preventing overtraining and facilitating adaptation.
General Aerobic:
~50–60% max effort, builds mileage safely without high intensity.
Recovery Runs:
~30–50% max effort, very light jog after a long run or hard session to promote blood flow, can add strides as well.

11. TAPERING
The taper is the period leading up to the race where you gradually reduce your training volume to allow your body to recover and prepare for peak performance.
A. Taper Duration: Typically, 2-3 weeks for a marathon.
B. Key Principles of Tapering:
Reduce Mileage: Gradually decrease weekly mileage, starting with a small reduction and progressively decreasing further each week.
Maintain Intensity: While reducing volume, maintain some intensity in workouts (e.g., shorter tempo runs or intervals) to keep legs sharp.
Focus on Rest and Recovery: Prioritize sleep, nutrition, and other recovery strategies during the taper.
C. Example Taper Schedule (3-Week Taper for Marathon):
Week 1: Reduce mileage by 20-25%.
Week 2: Reduce mileage by 40-50%.
Race Week: Significantly reduce mileage with only a few very short, easy runs leading up to the race.
D. Important Note on Tapering: Avoid hard workouts in the final week before the race. The goal is to arrive at the starting line feeling fresh and rested.
